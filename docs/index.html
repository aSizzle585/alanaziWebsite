Capstone Statement

SCLA 111 helped me see the deeper connections between language, culture, and identity. One of the biggest things I learned was how our cultural background shapes the way we communicate and understand each other. We explored themes like cultural identity, communication across different cultures, and what it means to be a global citizen. Through readings, class discussions, and writing assignments, I became more aware of my own perspective and how to listen to others with more empathy and curiosity.

This reflection shows how I have grown in expressing my ideas clearly and thinking more critically about the world around me. It is not just about writing better, it is about understanding people better. I have come out of this course more confident in my communication skills and more prepared to interact in diverse environments, whether that is in future classes, work, or everyday life.

Cultural and Language Profile

Growing up speaking both Arabic and English has always been a big part of who I am. I kind of live in two worlds, and honestly, that used to confuse me. Sometimes I felt like I had to be one version of myself when speaking Arabic with family and a whole different version when speaking English at school or with friends. In SCLA 111, we talked about code switching, and it finally clicked. I realized I am not being fake or trying to fit in. I am just adapting. It is actually kind of a superpower. I switch depending on who I am talking to, and that helps me connect with all kinds of people.

One thing that stuck with me from the class is how people treat languages differently. This idea of language ideology really made me think. Back home, English sometimes gets put on a pedestal, as if you speak it well, you are automatically smart or professional. And Arabic, even though it is my first language, can be looked down on in some academic spaces. That used to make me shy about speaking Arabic in public. But now, I get it. It is not the language that is the issue, it is the bias people have. Every language has its own beauty and depth. I am proud of both.

When it comes to cultural identity, I used to think I had to pick a side. Am I Saudi or am I someone studying in the US trying to fit into a different culture? Turns out I am both. And that is okay. I carry cultural values like hospitality, respect, and family loyalty with me everywhere. At the same time, being in the US taught me the value of speaking up, being independent, and thinking outside the box. I have learned to mix both sets of values depending on the situation.

One last thing I took from this class is intercultural competence. It is more than just knowing how to talk to people from different places. It is about listening, being curious, and not making assumptions. This class helped me become more aware of how I show up in conversations and how to be more open. My language and culture journey is still going, but now I feel more grounded in who I am and how I communicate.

Capstone Reflection

Throughout the past semester, I have learned many things and built upon lessons I gained from the mistakes I made last semester. However, my initial thought that this class was just an easy A meant to boost my GPA was one of the biggest assumptions I made, and I now see how wrong I was. This class has turned out to be one of the most reflective and meaningful courses I have ever taken. It really made me think about who I am, where I come from, and how that influences the way I see the world.

This realization began from day one when we all introduced ourselves and shared the places we came from. A big thing I noticed was the strong emphasis on learning about American values, but also connecting them to our own cultural and personal values. For example, in Project One, we were asked to create our own values that reflect our unique identities and backgrounds. That activity made me realize that we each carry a different set of beliefs and ideas shaped by where we were raised, the families we come from, and the cultures that surround us.

But that was only the beginning. Our professor continuously made the effort to relate our personal backgrounds to the topics we discussed in class. Whether it was in group discussions, lectures, or writing assignments, we were encouraged to think about how our own experiences shaped the way we interpreted the material. This class was not just about memorizing or understanding concepts. It was about learning to appreciate others, their cultures, and the different ways people see the world. Professor Bras created an environment that felt welcoming and respectful, and that made a huge difference.

In Project Two, we each picked an American value and, together with our team members, created a presentation showing our understanding of that value. I have always been someone who prefers to work alone. I usually try to avoid group work because I find it stressful trying to divide tasks or worrying whether the other people will do their part. At first, I still felt that way, and I did not enjoy it much. But this project helped me realize something important. My teammate turned out to be one of the most dependable and supportive people I have worked with. It reminded me of how Jem and Scout thought Boo Radley was a monster in To Kill a Mockingbird, but in reality, once they gave him a chance, he was just a quiet and kind man. I learned a similar lesson from this project— sometimes people surprise you in a good way if you just give them a chance.

Finally, we reached Project Three, the capstone of the semester. This project gave me a chance to reflect on all the work I have done and everything I have learned throughout the course. It brought everything together and made me realize just how much I have grown. I learned to reflect deeply, express myself more clearly, and connect with others in a more thoughtful and open way.

Even as I move on to my sophomore year, I hope to carry these lessons with me. I want to keep learning, growing, and becoming someone who listens and understands others better. I am thankful to my professor, Heejeon Bras, to Purdue University for giving me this opportunity, and to my country, Saudi Arabia, for making it possible for me to study here and gain such a valuable experience.
